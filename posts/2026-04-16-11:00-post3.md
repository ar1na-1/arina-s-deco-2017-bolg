# Designing the Interaction Flow for Goal-Oriented Feedback

## Problem Context

After defining the core feedback model in earlier design stages, the next challenge was organising how users move through the platform.

In a community environment, users may interact with the system in different ways. Some users browse existing posts and provide feedback, while others create new posts and request support from the community.

This means the interaction flow must support both reviewing and posting behaviour without creating confusion or unnecessary complexity.

## Problem Analysis

A key challenge was balancing structure and usability.

If the interaction flow is too simple, users may provide quick feedback without understanding the context of a post. This can lead to low-quality or irrelevant responses.

However, if the flow becomes too complex or contains too many steps, users may feel overwhelmed and stop participating.

Another challenge was role-switching. Within this platform, users can act as both:

- Content creators  
- Feedback reviewers  

The system therefore needed to support both behaviours within a single interface.

## Design Decision

To support these needs, the final interaction model was divided into two core flows.

### Flow 1: Reviewing Community Posts

Users who want to explore existing community content follow this path:

**Home Feed → View Post Detail → Submit Structured Feedback**

This flow allows users to first understand the context of a post before giving feedback. It ensures responses remain relevant to the creator’s stated goal.

### Flow 2: Creating a New Feedback Request

Users who want to share their own work follow this path:

**Create Post → Complete Feedback Goal Checklist → Publish Post**

This flow encourages users to clearly define what kind of feedback they need before sharing content with the community.

## Alternative Approaches

An alternative approach would have been allowing users to submit feedback directly from the home feed.

While this may reduce interaction time, it also removes important context, making feedback less reliable.

Another option would be a more complex multi-step evaluation form. While this may improve feedback quality, it would increase interaction cost and reduce participation.

## Trade-offs

The final interaction flow improves clarity and supports structured participation, but it introduces additional steps compared with open social platforms.

Users must enter a post detail page before responding, and creators must define a feedback goal before publishing.

However, these additional steps were considered necessary to support more meaningful and goal-oriented community interaction.

## Conclusion

Designing the interaction flow was essential for transforming the feedback model into an actual user experience.

By supporting both reviewing and posting behaviours through two clear pathways, the system creates a more focused, usable, and collaborative feedback environment.
