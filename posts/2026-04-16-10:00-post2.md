# Why Structured Feedback is Necessary in Goal-Oriented Community Interactions

## Problem Context

After defining the core functional requirements in the previous stage, the next challenge was determining how users should provide feedback once they enter a post.

In many online communities, users are able to leave comments freely. While this encourages participation, it often produces responses that are inconsistent, subjective, or unrelated to what the original poster actually wants to improve.

This becomes especially problematic when users are sharing unfinished work, prototypes, or early design concepts and need feedback on a specific design goal rather than general opinions.

## Problem Analysis

A key issue with free-form feedback is that reviewers often respond based on personal preference rather than the poster’s intended focus.

For example, if a user shares a mobile prototype and asks whether the navigation flow is easy to understand, reviewers may instead comment on colours, typography, or visual style.

Although these comments may still be useful, they do not directly help the user evaluate the design goal they are currently testing.

This creates two major problems:

- Feedback becomes difficult to compare across reviewers.
- Users struggle to identify which comments are actionable.

As a result, feedback may feel socially engaging, but not practically useful.

## Design Decision

To solve this issue, the prototype adopts a structured feedback model.

Instead of allowing unrestricted comments immediately, users are guided through three predefined prompts:

- What feels clear?
- What feels confusing?
- What could be improved?

These prompts were directly implemented in the Assignment 2 prototype within the post detail page.

This structure helps reviewers focus on observation, interpretation, and suggestion rather than personal preference alone. It also creates consistency across responses, making feedback easier for the original poster to review and compare.

## Prototype Integration

This decision was implemented in the interaction flow:

**Feed → View Post → Submit Structured Feedback**

When users open a post from the community feed, they are taken to a detailed post page where they can view the prototype, understand the stated feedback goal, and then complete the guided feedback prompts.

This ensures that feedback is given with sufficient context.

## Alternative Approaches

One alternative was allowing users to comment directly from the feed page. This would reduce interaction cost and increase speed. However, this approach would likely reduce engagement with the post content and increase the chance of low-context feedback.

Another alternative was using fully open text comments without prompts. While this would maximise flexibility, it would make feedback harder to compare and organise.

The chosen approach prioritises context and actionability over speed.

## Trade-offs

Structured feedback improves relevance and consistency, but it also introduces more effort compared to writing a quick comment.

Users must read the prompts, reflect on the content, and respond within a defined structure.

This may reduce casual participation, but it improves the overall quality of community interaction.

## Conclusion

Structured feedback is essential for supporting goal-oriented interaction in community systems.

By guiding users through specific prompts and embedding this structure into the prototype’s detail page, the platform supports feedback that is more contextual, interpretable, and actionable.
