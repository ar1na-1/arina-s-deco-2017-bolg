# Why Structured Feedback is Necessary in Goal-Oriented Community Interactions

## Problem Context

After defining the core functional requirements in the previous stage, the next challenge was determining how users should provide feedback once they enter a post.

In many online communities, users are able to leave comments freely. While this encourages participation, it often produces responses that are inconsistent, subjective, or unrelated to what the original poster actually wants to improve.

This becomes especially problematic when users are sharing unfinished work, prototypes, or early design concepts and need feedback on a specific design goal rather than general opinions.

## Problem Analysis

A key issue with free-form feedback is that reviewers often respond based on personal preference rather than the poster’s intended focus.

For example, if a user shares a mobile prototype and asks whether the navigation flow is easy to understand, reviewers may instead comment on colours, typography, or visual style.

Although these comments may still be useful, they do not directly help the user evaluate the design goal they are currently testing.

This creates two major problems:

- Feedback becomes difficult to compare across reviewers.
- Users struggle to identify which comments are actionable.

As a result, feedback may feel socially engaging, but not practically useful.

## Design Decision

To solve this issue, the prototype adopts a structured feedback model.

Instead of allowing unrestricted comments immediately, users are guided through three predefined prompts:

- **What feels clear?**
- **What feels confusing?**
- **What could be improved?**

These prompts were directly implemented in the Assignment 2 prototype within the post detail page.

This structure helps reviewers focus on observation, interpretation, and suggestion rather than personal preference alone.

It also creates consistency across responses, making feedback easier for the original poster to review and compare.
